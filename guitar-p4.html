How to Use This Tool
  1. Pick a key — Click any wedge on the Circle of Fifths to choose your root note.
  2. Choose a mode — Select one of the seven modes. The app defaults to Ionian (major scale).
  3. Explore the scale — The scale notes appear in the info panel. Click any note to hear it. The root note is highlighted in red.
  4. Play chords — The diatonic chords for your key and mode are shown with Roman numerals. Click a chord card to hear its notes played as a short arpeggio.
  5. Use the fretboard — The guitar neck shows 15 frets in all-fourths tuning (E A D G C F). Scale tones are highlighted and labeled; the root note gets a stronger highlight. Click or tap any position to hear it.
All Fourths Tuning

All-fourths tuning (also called P4 tuning) tunes every string a perfect fourth apart: E A D G C F (low to high).

Standard tuning is almost all fourths — E A D G B E — but the interval between the 3rd (G) and 2nd (B) strings is a major third instead of a fourth. All-fourths tuning removes that exception, making the interval uniform across all six strings.

Advantages

  • Uniform patterns — Every scale shape, arpeggio, and chord voicing works identically on every pair of adjacent strings. Learn a pattern once and move it anywhere on the neck without adjustment.
  • Easier transposition — Shift any shape up or down the neck, or across string groups, and it stays correct. No more memorizing separate shapes for the B-string offset.
  • Simpler intervals — Interval geometry is consistent: an octave is always two strings up and two frets over, a fifth is always one string up and two frets over, etc.

Trade-offs

  • Standard chord voicings change — Open chords and barre chords from standard tuning no longer apply on the top two strings. Many familiar shapes need reworking.
  • Slightly higher top strings — The 1st string is an F instead of an E, one semitone higher in tension. Some players use a lighter gauge on top.
  • Less learning material — Most guitar instruction assumes standard tuning, so you'll be translating or figuring things out on your own.

Players like Stanley Jordan and Tom Quayle are well-known advocates of all-fourths tuning. It's particularly popular among jazz and fusion guitarists who value the symmetry for improvisation.

The Circle of Fifths

The Circle of Fifths arranges all twelve musical keys so that each one is a perfect fifth (seven semitones) above the previous. Moving clockwise adds one sharp; moving counter-clockwise adds one flat.

  • Clockwise (sharps): C → G → D → A → E → B → F#
  • Counter-clockwise (flats): C → F → Bb → Eb → Ab → Db → Gb

Adjacent keys on the circle share most of their notes, which makes them natural choices for key changes. For example, C major and G major differ by only one note (F vs F#). This is why the circle is so useful for songwriting and improvisation—it maps harmonic distance.

Understanding Modes

Modes are seven different scales you can build from the same set of notes by starting on a different degree. Each mode has a distinct mood or color:

Ionian (Major)
The familiar major scale. Bright, happy, resolved. Think: "Happy Birthday," most pop music.
Dorian
A minor scale with a raised 6th. Jazzy, smooth, sophisticated. Think: "So What" by Miles Davis, funk and soul.
Phrygian
A minor scale with a lowered 2nd. Dark, exotic, Spanish-flavored. Think: flamenco guitar, metal intros.
Lydian
A major scale with a raised 4th. Dreamy, floating, ethereal. Think: film scores, "The Simpsons" theme.
Mixolydian
A major scale with a lowered 7th. Bluesy, laid-back, dominant. Think: classic rock, blues, "Norwegian Wood."
Aeolian (Natural Minor)
The natural minor scale. Sad, serious, introspective. Think: "Losing My Religion," most minor-key pop.
Locrian
A minor scale with lowered 2nd and 5th. Unstable, dissonant, tense. Rarely used as a home key; useful for creating tension over diminished chords.
Diatonic Chords & Roman Numerals

When you build a triad on each degree of a scale using only notes from that scale, you get the diatonic chords. Roman numerals label each chord by its position:

  • Uppercase (I, IV, V) = major chord
  • Lowercase (ii, iii, vi) = minor chord
  • Lowercase with ° (vii°) = diminished chord

In the key of C Ionian (major), the chords are: C – Dm – Em – F – G – Am – Bdim. Common progressions like I–V–vi–IV (C–G–Am–F) come from combining these chords. Each mode rearranges which degrees are major, minor, or diminished, giving you different harmonic palettes to improvise over.

Reading the Fretboard

The fretboard is displayed in all-fourths tuning (low E to high F, bottom to top). Fret 0 is the open string, and frets are numbered left to right.

  • Highlighted dots show notes that belong to the current scale.
  • Red dots mark the root note — these are your "home base" positions for the selected key.
  • Dot markers below the fretboard appear at frets 3, 5, 7, 9, 12 (double dot), and 15, just like on a real guitar neck.

Because every string pair uses the same interval, any scale pattern you learn can be moved directly to any string group without modification. Look for how the shapes repeat uniformly — this is the core benefit of P4 tuning.

Tip: The 12th fret is one octave above the open string — the pattern of notes repeats from there. Use this to learn the neck in two halves.
